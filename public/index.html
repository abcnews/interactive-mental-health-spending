Far from care

By Annika Blau, Joshua Byrd and Georgina Piper from the ABC News Story Lab Mon 16 Nov 2020, 5:53 PM AEDT

How your postcode can influence whether you need help — and if you'll get it.

If there's one thing we can all agree on, it's that 2020 has been a rough year.

With the pandemic taking a toll on our mental health, it's worth asking, can Australians get help when they need it?

Australians mostly access mental health care through the Medicare system, with taxpayers spending $1.3 billion a year on rebates.

But is help getting where it's needed most?

Who needs help?

This line tracks the proportion of people experiencing a high level of psychological distress (outside of the pandemic).

On the left we have the most disadvantaged fifth of Australians and on the right we have the least disadvantaged.

In the most disadvantaged suburbs, 18.3 per cent of people are highly distressed compared with 9 per cent in the least disadvantaged.

As you can see, people in advantaged areas are generally less distressed, which makes sense.

They also have lower rates of mental and behavioural conditions.

So you might expect the bulk of our tax dollars to be subsidising care for people living on the other end of the spectrum.

Let's show you just how far off the mark that is.

Where your tax dollars are going

Each of these dots is an area of Australia, grouped into six major categories.

The chart shows how much taxpayers are spending on mental health appointments for each area (excluding psychiatrists and GP care, which we'll come back to).

This dotted line shows the trend using the average of each group — it's what you want to pay attention to.

Taxpayers spent the most per person on Darebin-South in Melbourne's northern suburbs and the least on the NT region of Katherine, often described as "where the outback meets the tropics". In fact, we spent 33 times more per person on Darebin-South than Katherine.

In Darebin-South, we spent $4912 on 46.49 sessions of care per 100 people in 2017-18. In Katherine, we spent just $147 for 1.49 sessions.

This allowed 8.94 per cent of people in Darebin-South to access subsidised care, compared with 0.45 per cent in Katherine.

Now Darebin-South might be the highest spend, but it's a bit of an outlier for its category.

If we look at the average line, we can see we're generally spending the most on highly advantaged city areas.

Spending is lowest in remote areas, and everywhere else falls in the middle.

In fact, taxpayers are funnelling five times as much dosh to highly advantaged city slickers as they are to remote Australians, and people in the regions are missing out too.

Cathy* is just a few hours from inner-city Sydney, but in her neck of the woods, you're half as likely to see a rebated clinical psychologist as in the city.

"It took over six weeks after a particular crisis before I was able to see a psychologist. I just had to soldier on, which is very dangerous in a mental health crisis.

"It was pointless to go to the local hospital. I would've been deemed 'not sick enough' or sent away to Goulburn, where [people in my situation] can't have family support if they need it. So I just had to wait. I pretty much just went to bed for six weeks.

"The crackdown on opioid painkillers is just one issue in rural areas where mental health care is lacking. Add to that the ongoing trauma of the bushfires in my area and now the Covid-19 pandemic."

But it's not just country folk losing out. Even in cities, for every dollar we spend on those in highly advantaged areas, we spend 76 cents on people in low-advantage areas.

Mike* lives in one of those less advantaged city areas — Wollongong.

"My quality of life goes up significantly when I can have a session every fortnight, then it goes right down again when I can't. When I'm doing well I can work a bit and take care of myself. When I'm not well I can't leave the house, can't sleep, can't care for myself or my child. It's cost me years of my life, struggling, being poor, unable to access proper health care and feeling like a useless burden on society.

"It'd cost about $5000 a year for me to see my psychologist fortnightly. Contrast this against the $20,000 or so I get for the disability pension every year. If they helped me get on my feet and supported me to stay there I'd cost the taxpayers way less, and I'd be a contributing member of society."

The most unequal care of all

Nowhere is the inequality more pronounced than when it comes to psychiatrists in private practice.

For every dollar taxpayers spend on psychiatry for remote Australians, we shell out $3.45 for people in disadvantaged city areas and $7.70 for people in our most advantaged suburbs.

ABC reader Laura* lives in a medium-advantage area and says even with a "well-paying job", the upfront cost to see a psychiatrist is prohibitive.

"I was referred to ensure that my antidepressants were the right ones for me but the appointments were $400.

"I nearly had another breakdown, I couldn't get the help I needed. I was trying to improve my mental health, I didn't want to be in the dark place anymore. I wanted to continue in life."

Psychiatrists and clinical psychologists are the most specialised and highly trained professionals in the mental health care system, but this data suggests they're accessible for a select few only.

For every person who sees a clinical psychologist in a remote area, six people will be accessing care in a highly-advantaged city area.

We can see that people from all backgrounds are seeking help by looking at who's visiting GPs for mental health reasons.

But for less advantaged or more remote Australians, the journey often seems to end in their GP's office.

"While wealthier Australians are often getting access to quality care that combines psychology and medication, poorer Australians are often left with only medication from their GP, which can put them on the back foot with recovery," says Professor Graham Meadows from Monash University's Department of Psychiatry.

So what's going on?

The number of Australians getting psychological care has exploded since Medicare rebates were introduced in 2006. Some 1.32 million Australians have a mental health care plan from their GP — a piece of paper that opens up subsidised sessions with psychologists and other professionals.

Others are seeing psychiatrists in private practice and getting a portion back from Medicare.

Many ABC readers told us this support has been "life-changing". As Caitlan from eastern Sydney put it, "I feel so lucky to have accessed this service which allowed me to build a stable life. I can travel, I have friends, I have familial relationships — I owe all that to receiving the help I did."

But whether you can access this care remains heavily influenced by your postcode.

More than 600 Australians shared their experiences with us, and the number one barrier they cited was the cost of appointments.

The recommended rate for a psychologist is $260 per session, while an initial consult with a psychiatrist costs around $400 (further appointments become cheaper). You only get a portion back from Medicare and the rebate can take a couple of days.

Many Australians simply can't cough up those fees and bulk-billing psychologists are rare and usually have long waiting lists.

To access psychological care, you need to get a mental health care plan, which involves a double appointment with a GP at the start and a review after the sixth psychology session. If you don't have a bulk-billing GP, that's the cost of three to four doctor's appointments on top of the psychology fees.

The number of one-on-one psychology sessions you can receive a rebate on has just been increased from a maximum of 10 per year to 20, in response to the pandemic. (People with eating disorders have a special exemption and can access up to 40 sessions). Beyond this, you're paying full fees. Before these extra sessions were introduced, many readers told us the 10-session cap was limiting their recovery.

The extra sessions are only available until June 2022, despite the Government's own Medicare taskforce recommending that people should be able to access up to 40 sessions, depending on their needs.

Lifting the 10-session cap would unlock forms of therapy that take longer but produce better results for some conditions, like complex trauma and borderline personality disorder, which can't usually be treated in 10 sessions.

"We need to acknowledge that for some members of our community, chronic health issues require ongoing access to support," says Dr Simon Knowles, senior lecturer in psychology at Swinburne University.

But increasing the number of rebated sessions won't help those Australians who can't afford the gap fee to begin with.

Most psychologists charge a gap fee as they say the Medicare rebate doesn't come close to covering their costs. The rebate hasn't increased since 2012, yet the costs of running a practice are always going up, according to the Australian Psychological Society — which is passed on to patients.

"Telehealth could lower gap fees by removing some of the costs of running a physical practice — but there's no guarantee those savings would be passed on to patients," says Professor Meadows.

"To make it a truly universal system, you'd need to do something dramatic — maybe lift the rebate to a reasonable level and then make gap fees illegal."

"Or, instead of having this fee-for-service model of privatised care, you put the almost-billion dollars a year of funding into bulk-billing psychological services in every area."

Currently, some vulnerable groups can access free mental health services through their local Primary Health Network or charities — but for everyone else, there's not much between the Medicare system and a hospital emergency department.

Therapy takes time

Money isn't the only barrier. As they say, time is money, and "lower-income Australians typically have greater demands on their time", according to Jennifer Smith-Merry, director of the Centre for Disability Research and Policy at the University of Sydney.

Like most Australians, psychologists work during business hours with limited after-hours sessions that can fill up quickly.

They're clustered in advantaged city areas, according to the National Mental Health Commission, so everyone else must hike across town, which can take longer than the appointment itself.

I have a dysfunctional manager who, despite me repeatedly reminding them I will be leaving early on day X, manages to find an onerous task that absolutely must be done RIGHT NOW, meaning I miss my appointment. This happened to about 50 per cent of my appointments in 2018, and about 30 per cent in 2019.

I had a breakdown one time and just sat on the footpath outside work trying to cry quietly and stop my mascara running so I wouldn't be found out. The disappointment was soul crushing, I felt so alone. I had to wait another 4-6 weeks and at the time, I didn't think I would last that long.

Again, telehealth could help — as a working mother from Boroondara told us, "it's far more convenient for people like me juggling many demands. The time taken out of 'life' is only for the session, not all the other travel time". The Productivity Commission has recommended telehealth as a "priority reform", but the Government has previously resisted calls to offer it outside of the pandemic.

But does mental health care have to involve an hour on the couch? Dr Sebastian Rosenberg from the University of Sydney has argued that free online therapy courses with a variety of durations that are available at any time are accessible for more Australians. Online programs can also be made culturally sensitive, getting around the fact that psychologists are overwhelmingly white.

However, many psychologists feel online programs are inadequate, saying the most important thing for healing is the personal relationship between therapist and client.

Still, it's clear the one-size-fits-all approach is failing to serve Australians from all backgrounds.

"There's growing discussions overseas about how psychologists are largely from affluent backgrounds, university-educated and from inner-city areas" says Professor Meadows.

"You can see how someone from a different background might not feel a connection with that kind of person. So we need to recognise that different groups need different approaches."

Affordable care exists, but there's a catch

We've looked at the providers where care is most unequal, but what about those that are doing better?

Let's return to our dots for a second.

The only services that aren't heavily skewed towards more advantaged city folk are provided by "other" allied mental health workers — a group that includes mental health nurses, Aboriginal health workers and some accredited social workers and occupational therapists.

We spend so little on this group of health providers that we need to zoom in to see the trends.

Finally, we see a flattening of the curve, with the exception of the most remote Australians.

The reason these providers are more accessible is because rather than operating in private practice, they're usually employed by organisations whose mandate is to reach disadvantaged or isolated areas.

But unfortunately, very few people are receiving these services.

For example, we're spending the most per person in WA's Wheat Belt - South region, yet this is helping just 1.14 per cent of people.

Some GPs also have special training to offer family or individual therapy, but this is used even less. While this could be a way of offering accessible therapy for low-income families, just 15,655 people accessed this care in 2017-18.

So, while more equitable care exists, it's not being widely deployed.

Compounding the problem, Medicare offers no rebate to see counsellors or other therapists, who typically have much lower fees than psychologists.

This is designed to prioritise forms of care with the strongest evidence base. But the exclusion means a large chunk of Australia's mental health workforce is effectively underutilised, while patients are locked into care they often can't afford beyond 10 sessions.

Making matters worse, organisations who traditionally employed psychologists (like hospitals, companies and universities) realised over the years that they could save money by sending people to the Medicare system instead, where consumers must foot the gap fee, according to the Australian Psychological Society. The peak body says this practice of "cost shifting" has seen the number of psychologists employed by organisations shrink.

How do we burst the city bubble?

So what would it take to make access to psychologists look less like this?

To reverse the city skew, we could start by improving the incentives for psychologists and psychiatrists to practice in regional areas.

We could also work to remove the barriers to telehealth for country Australians, like variable internet and having to visit a GP in person for a referral. As one reader from the NT put it, "the nearest GP is a nine-hour drive — it was discouraging and made me feel even more isolated."

Rather than expecting the current system to work everywhere, we need to tailor services to the realities of country areas, according to Dr Smith-Merry, because "people with disadvantage are more likely to live there".

'It saved me'

As bleak as 2020 has been, it also presents an opportunity for reform, according to Dr Knowles.

"The pandemic has no doubt made us all reflect on mental health and where changes can and should be made to help improve access to support."

From the hundreds of testimonials you sent us, one theme was clear: when you were able to access care, it was often "life-changing".

About the data:

  • The proportion of Australians who are highly distressed and the proportion experiencing a mental or behavioural condition come from the 2017-18 National Health Survey (Australian Bureau of Statistics).

    • Proportions are shown by Index of Relative Socio-Economic Disadvantage quintile. Lower quintiles are more disadvantaged while higher quintiles are less disadvantaged.
    • The "highly distressed" proportions include those who recorded a "high" or "very high" level of distress using the Kessler 10 scale in the National Health Survey.

  • The Medicare rebates and service usage by area data comes from the AIHW analysis, Medicare-subsidised GP, allied health and specialist health care across local areas, 2013–14 to 2017–18.

    • The charts use 2017-18 data only and show Medicare rebates per 100 people by ABS Statistical Area 3 (SA3).
    • Further information about the AIHW analysis can be found in its technical note.
