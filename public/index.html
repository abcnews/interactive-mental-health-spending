Access denied

Mon 14 Sep 2020, 4:00 PM AEST

If there’s one thing we can all agree on, it’s that 2020 has been a rough year.

It started with the country in flames before shifting from one apocalyptic scenario to the next as coronavirus reached our shores.

With Australia staring down a “mental health pandemic”, it’s worth asking, is our system equipped to cope?

Besides landing in an emergency department, most Australians access mental health care through the Medicare system.

You might be one of the approximately 1.32 million Australians who’ve been given a mental health plan by your GP - a piece of paper that opens up Medicare-rebated sessions with psychologists and other professionals. Or you may have seen a psychiatrist in private practice.

Taxpayers are spending $1.3 billion each year on Medicare rebates for mental health care, so it’s time we asked - is help getting to those who need it most?

We’re about to show you some fancy charts, but to make them more relevant for you we’ll need your postcode, or a postcode you’re curious about. Don’t worry, we’re not storing or re-using this information.

Is your area accessing care?

This year, we’ve all become experts in reading curvy lines. But we’re about to show you a curve you might not have seen before.

This line tracks the proportion of people experiencing a high level of psychological distress.

On the left we have the most disadvantaged fifth of Australians and on the right we have the least disadvantaged.

As you can see, distress rises with disadvantage. It makes sense that if you’re struggling to put food on the table and make rent, you’re likely to be distressed.

And it’s not just distress - disadvantaged Australians have higher rates of mental and behavioural conditions also.

So you might expect the bulk of our tax dollars to be subsidising care for these Australians. The sicker you are, the more likely you are to be seeing someone about it, right?

Wrong. Let's show you just how wrong.

We've crunched the numbers to see who’s getting access to Medicare-rebated care, which is how most Australians access mental health services outside of landing in an emergency room.

This chart shows where we’re spending the most on allied mental health services through Medicare (that’s care provided by psychologists and other mental health professionals, excluding psychiatrists).

Each of those dots is an area of Australia, grouped into six major categories.

In your area of <<SYDNEY INNER CITY>>, taxpayers funded <<25.6 sessions>> of care per 100 people, which cost <<$2,790>>. That’s <<more>> than the national average of 22.87 sessions for $2,375.

This allowed <<5.08%>> of people in your area to access subsidised mental health care, which is <<around>> the national average of 5.06%.

As you can see, we’re spending the most on areas in major cities with a high level of advantage.

Spending is lowest in remote areas, and everywhere else falls in the middle.

In fact, taxpayers are funneling five times as much dosh to highly advantaged city slickers as they are to remote Australians, and people in the regions are missing out too.

Kaye lives on NSW’s South Coast. She's just a few hours from inner-city Sydney, but in her neck of the woods, the proportion of people who get funding to see a psychologist is half what it is in the big smoke.

"I just had to soldier on"

It took over 6 weeks after a particular crisis before I was able to see a psychologist. I just had to soldier on, which is very dangerous in a mental health crisis.

Living in a rural community, my options are limited. I knew it was pointless to go to hospital. I would have been deemed not 'sick' enough or sent away to Goulburn. People with severe or persistent mental health problems have to throw themselves on the mercy of the local hospital which has a very small, often understaffed mental health ward, with fly in/fly out psychiatrists. Mostly, these desperate clients are sent away to a bigger facility which means they cannot have family support if they need it.

So I just had to wait. I pretty much just went to bed for six weeks.

The crackdown on opioid painkillers is just one issue in rural areas where mental health care is lacking. Add to that the ongoing trauma of the bushfires in my area and now the Covid-19 pandemic - it worries me greatly.

But it’s not just country folk losing out. For every dollar we spend on wealthier city-siders, we spend just 77 cents on people in less advantaged city areas.

Mike* lives in one of those areas - Wollongong.

"It's cost years of my life"

I’m on a disability pension because I have bipolar 2 disorder. My quality of life goes up significantly when I can have a session every fortnight, then it goes right down again when I can’t. When I’m doing well I can work a bit and take care of myself. When I’m not well I can’t leave the house, can’t sleep, can’t care for myself or my child. It’s cost me years of my life, struggling, being poor, unable to access proper health care and feeling like a useless burden on society.

It’d cost about $5000 a year for me to see my psychologist fortnightly. Contrast this against the $20,000 or so I get for the disability pension every year. If they helped me get on my feet and supported me to stay there I’d cost the taxpayers way less, and I’d be a contributing member of society.

THE WORST OFFENDERS

As we can see, it’s an unequal system, and if we look beyond allied health to psychiatrists, things get even worse.

For every dollar taxpayers spend on psychiatry for remote Australians, we shell out $4.80 for people in disadvantaged city areas and $7.70 for people in our wealthiest suburbs.

It’s a similar story when we check out clinical psychologists.

For every person who sees a clinical psychologist in a remote area, six people will be accessing care in a highly-advantaged city area.

Psychiatrists and clinical psychologists are the most specialised and highly trained professionals in the mental health care system but this data suggests they’re accessible for a select few only.

We can tell that Australians from all backgrounds are seeking help by looking at who’s visiting GPs for mental health reasons. While there’s still fewer remote Australians seeking help, the curve is flatter than other providers.

But for less advantaged Australians, the journey seems to end in their GP’s office.

“While wealthier Australians are getting access to care, poorer Australians are left with only medication from their GP and the research shows medication alone is less effective in treating mental illness in the long-term, which puts them on the back foot in terms of recovery,” says Professor Graham Meadows from Monash University’s Department of Psychiatry.

So what’s going on?

‘You don’t send someone with cancer home after ten appointments’

More than 600 Australians shared their experiences of accessing mental health care through Medicare with us, and the number one barrier cited was the cost of appointments.

The recommended rate for a psychologist is $260 per session, while an initial consult with a psychiatrist will set you back around $400. You can get a portion of the cost back from Medicare, but you need to have the full amount upfront and the rebate can take a couple of days.

Many Australians simply can’t cough up those fees and bulk-billing psychologists are rare, with long waiting lists. And while you can have an unlimited number of rebated sessions with a psychiatrist, rebates on a psychologist are capped at 10 individual and 10 group sessions a year.

To access those 10 sessions, you typically need a mental health care plan from a GP which involves a long appointment (double the cost), plus a review after the sixth session. That’s the cost of three to four appointments before you even get to the psychologist’s fees.

There’s just not enough sessions to truly get into my trauma. I have borderline personality disorder and complex post traumatic stress disorder. I can’t fix this in 10 visits. When COViD happened I had to take stress leave and was almost hospitalised. I was suicidal. The system might be OK for people with depression and anxiety but it isn’t set up for people with complex mental health needs. Some people have told me to apply for the NDIS but I’m too exhausted to battle another government system. It’s very hard to be mentally unwell in Australia. There is almost no support.

Both the Productivity Commission and the Government’s Medicare taskforce think Australians should be able to access more subsidised sessions.

The taskforce has recommended that people have up to 40 subsidised sessions a year, depending on the severity of their condition. Healthy people “at risk” of mental illness should also be able to access rebates, the taskforce says, which are currently only available after you’ve developed a mental condition.

Lifting the 10-session cap would allow psychologists to use forms of therapy that take longer but produce better results for some conditions, like complex trauma and borderline personality disorder, which can’t be treated with the kind of therapy you can deliver in 10 sessions.

But increasing the number of rebated sessions wouldn’t help those Australians who can’t afford the gap fee to begin with.

Psychologists say they have to charge a gap fee as the Medicare rebate doesn’t come close to covering their costs. The rebate hasn’t increased since 2012, yet the costs of running a practice increase every year — which is passed on to patients.

“To make it a truly universal system, you’d need to lift the rebate to a reasonable level and then make gap fees illegal,” says Professor Meadows.

“Or, instead of having this fee-for-service model of privatised care, you put the almost-billion dollars a year of funding into bulk-billing centres in every area.”

“With the current model, telehealth could lower gap fees by removing some of the costs of running a physical practice - but there’s no guarantee those savings would be passed on to patients.”

TIME

But money isn’t the only barrier. As they say, time is money, and “lower-income Australians typically have greater demands on their time,” according to Jennifer Smith-Merry, director of the Centre for Disability Research and Policy at the University of Sydney.

Like most Australians, psychologists work during business hours with limited after-hours sessions that can fill up quickly.

They prefer to operate in wealthier, city areas where people are more able to cop a gap fee, meaning everyone else must hike across town, which can take longer than the appointment itself.

There’s a possible solution — telehealth — which the Productivity Commission recommended this month. Yet the Government has long-resisted calls to offer this outside of the pandemic.

Does mental health care always have to involve an hour on the couch? Dr Sebastian Rosenberg from the University of Sydney has argued free online therapy courses with a variety of durations that could be accessed at any time would be accessible for more Australians. Online programs could also be made culturally sensitive, getting around the fact that psychologists are overwhelmingly white.

But many psychologists feel online programs are inadequate, saying the most important thing for healing is the personal relationship between therapist and client.

Still, it’s clear that the one-size-fits-all approach is failing to serve Australians from all backgrounds.

“There’s growing discussions overseas about how psychologists are largely from affluent backgrounds, atheist, university-educated and from inner-city areas” says Professor Meadows.

“You can see how someone from a different background might not feel a connection with that kind of person. So we need to recognise that different groups need different approaches.”

DISTANCE

We’ve looked at the providers where care is most unequal, but what about those that are doing better?

Let’s return to our dots for a second.

The rebates given to this group of health providers is so small, we need to zoom in to make out the pile of dots

The only services that aren’t heavily skewed towards more advantaged city folk are provided by “other” allied mental health workers — a group that includes mental health nurses, Aboriginal health workers and some accredited social workers and occupational therapists.

Finally, we see a flattening of the curve - with the exception of the most remote Australians.

The reason these providers are more accessible is because rather than operating in private practice, they’re usually employed by organisations whose mandate is to reach disadvantaged or isolated areas.

Unfortunately, very few people are receiving these services.

In your area of <<SYDNEY INNER CITY>>, just <<1%>> of people received this kind of care, compared with <<5%>> who saw a clinical psychologist.

Some GPs also have special training to offer family or individual therapy, but this is used even less. While this could be a way of offering accessible therapy for low-income families, just over 16,000 people access this care yearly.

This is designed to prioritise forms of care with the strongest evidence base. But the exclusion means a large chunk of Australia’s mental health workforce is effectively underutilised, while patients are locked into care they can’t afford beyond 10 sessions.

Well to start with, we could introduce formal incentives for psychologists to work in country areas, like we have with doctors.

In 2017, rural and remote Australians became able to access sessions through telehealth, but some barriers remain. Not everyone is comfortable doing therapy by videoconference (phone isn’t an option) and not everywhere has a stable internet connection.

Plus, some readers told us they still had to drive hours to see a GP for their mental health care plan and review, which could be resolved by offering this through telehealth.

I've lived in a remote community for the past couple of years. Earlier this year, I tried to access a mental health care plan through a telehealth service, but was told that a referral was not available except by visiting a GP in person first. The nearest GP is a 9 hour drive. It was very discouraging and made me feel even more isolated. I waited until I had some leave and a chance to visit town, accessing three sessions in two weeks with a psychologist before returning to work in the community, where I was able to continue sessions through telehealth. There seem to be even fewer avenues for mental health support for Aboriginal people living in remote areas.

Alternatively, other medical professionals besides GPs could give referrals in country areas. “Any door should be the right door to get help,” says Dr Smith-Merry.

“We treat australia as a monolith despite us all knowing that you can’t operate the same models in rural and regional Australia because those services just aren’t there.

“Better Access was not really built with the different types of approaches you need in those areas. Yet people with disadvantage are more likely to live there.”

THERE’S NEVER BEEN A BETTER TIME FOR REFORM
